    • neck pain

  • Besides taking pain relievers, self-care measures that might relieve neck pain include: Alternate heat and cold.
  • Reduce inflammation by applying cold, such as an ice pack or ice wrapped in a towel, for up to 15 minutes several times a day during the first 48 hours.
  • After that, use heat.
  • Ice: Apply an ice pack to numb the area and soothe inflamed muscles. Ice reduces inflammation.
  • Heat: After a day or two, apply a heating pad or warm compress. Heat relaxes the stiff neck muscles.

    • Trouble breathing

  • Avoid food and drink: Do not give the person food or drink.
  • Avoid moving the person: Do not move the person if there has been a head, neck, chest, or airway injury, unless it is absolutely necessary. If the person must be moved, protect and stabilize the neck.
  • Position: Sitting forward in a chair can help relieve breathlessness. To do this, sit in a comfortable chair, and lean forward a little. Your feet should be flat and a comfortable distance apart. Rest your elbows on your thighs and relax your shoulders as much as possible.
  • CPR: Open the airway using the head-tilt, chin-lift maneuverer. Pinch the nostrils shut for mouth-to-mouth breathing and cover the person's mouth with yours, making a seal. Give the first rescue breath, lasting one second, and watch to see if the chest rises. If it rises, give the second breath.
  • Turning the person: If you suspect a back or neck injury, you should still move them on to their side so they can keep breathing. Try to keep their spine in a straight line when turning them. If possible, get someone to help you turn them.

    • Decreased ability to exercise

  • Drink water or orange juiceFuel your body. Drink plenty of water before, during, and after you work out. Get off to a good start by drinking about 17 to 20 ounces of water about 2 or 3 hours before exercising. A good general rule is to then drink this amount of water:
    8 ounces about 20 to 30 minutes before working out
    8 ounces every 10 to 20 minutes during your workout
    8 ounces within a half hour of when your workout is done
  • Warm-up and cool-down. Every workout should begin with a warm-up and end with a cool-down period. A warm-up helps your body get ready for exercise. It gradually increases your heart rate and loosens your muscles and joints. Some ways to warm up: Ride an exercise bike
    Jump rope
    Jog in place for 5 to 10 minutes
    A cool-down after you work out is important to slowly bring your heart rate back to normal. Walking for 5 to 10 minutes after you work out is one way to cool down.
  • Stretch. Do dynamic stretching before and after you work out. This will help increase flexibility. Research is conflicting as to whether it can also help prevent injury, it’s best to stretch after you warm up and cool down.
  • Ease into it. When you begin an exercise routine or start a new workout program, start slowly. Then gradually build up the intensity, duration, and frequency.

    • Cough

  • Drink fluids: Offer breast milk or formula to babies, and cool water and juice to older children. Avoid carbonated or citrus drinks, which can irritate a raw throat.
  • Use a humidifier: Run a cool-mist humidifier in your child's bedroom. You can also use a humidifier to make the air moist, or breathe steam from a hot shower or teakettle before bed.
  • Gargle salt water: Gargle with warm salt water regularly to remove mucus and soothe your throat.
  • Avoid irritants: Avoid smoke and dust.
  • Use saline nose drops: Use saline (saltwater) nose drops to relieve congestion.
  • Use warm fluids: The warmth of a drink like tea or lemon water can soothe a throat that feels raw or sore from coughing. Warm fluids also help to loosen mucus.
  • Use honey: Honey, hard candies, or cough drops can help relieve a sore throat caused by coughing.

    • Chronic fatigue

  • Avoid inflammatory foods, limit caffeine, and try smaller, more frequent meals. You can also try keeping a food and symptom journal.
  • Stay hydrated.
  • Try deep breathing, muscle relaxation, massage, and movement therapies like stretching, yoga, and tai chi. You can also try talking therapies like counseling or cognitive behavioral therapy.
  • Encourage frequent rest periods and help set priorities so that essential tasks get done. You can also work with a doctor to determine the best way to balance rest and activity.
  • Try to go to bed and get up at the same time every day. Lack of sleep can make other symptoms more difficult to deal with.
  • Modest exercise may worsen fatigue in many people with ME/CFS. It's important to exercise within your limits.

  • Avoid eating and drinking: Avoid eating and drinking for two hours after the last episode of vomiting.
  • Eat bland foods: Eat bland foods, such as rice, pasta, and crackers, and avoid fatty food.
  • Give them boiled water that has been cooled.

    • The painful leg is swollen or red

  • Elevation: Raise your legs above your heart for 30 minutes, three to four times a day. Gravity can help move fluids out of your legs and toward the rest of your body.
  • SCompression: Wrap an elastic bandage around your legs or wear compression stockings.
  • Ice: Apply ice immediately after an injury to reduce swelling. You can use ice packs, cold therapy systems, ice baths, or cryotherapy chambers. Apply ice for 20 minutes out of every hour for the first 72 hours. Leaving ice on longer may increase swelling.
  • Rest: Rest helps your ligament regain its shape.
  • Exercise: Exercise your legs to help pump fluid from your legs back to your heart.
  • ADiet: Follow a low-salt diet to reduce fluid buildup and swelling.

  • If possible, prop up the bruised area on pillows for the next few days. Try to keep the bruise above the level of your heart.
  • Avoid heat for the first 24–48 hours to avoid increasing the extent of bleeding and swelling.

    • sprain

  • Avoid activities that cause pain, swelling, or discomfort. Stop exercising or other activities, and try not to put any weight on the injury. Rest the injured area until it's less painful.
  • Apply an ice pack, a slush bath, or a compression sleeve filled with cold water to the area as soon as possible after the injury. Continue icing for 15 to 20 minutes, four to eight times a day, for the first 48 hours or until swelling improves.
  • Wrap a bandage around the injury to support it. Compress or bandage the injured site firmly, extending the wrapping from below to above.
  • Elevate the injured area above heart height whenever practical.

    • muscle spasms

  • Change position: Moving out of the position that's causing the spasm can help.
  • Stretch: Stretching relaxes the muscle and helps relieve contractions.
  • Heat and cold therapy: Applying heat soon after the spasm starts can help.
  • Self-massage: Massage can help relieve muscle spasms.
  • Hydration: Staying hydrated can help relieve muscle spasms.

    • bone Pain

  • Immobilize the area: Don't try to realign the bone. If you've been trained in how to splint, apply a splint to the area above and below the fracture sites. Padding the splints can help reduce discomfort.
  • Stop any bleeding: If they're bleeding, elevate and apply pressure to the wound.

    • Joint Pain

  • Rest: Try to rest the affected joint.
  • Ice: Apply an ice pack or bag of frozen peas wrapped in a towel to the painful area for up to 20 minutes every 2 to 3 hours.
  • Painkillers: Take painkillers, such as ibuprofen or paracetamol, but do not take ibuprofen in the first 48 hours after an injury.
  • Topical pain relievers: Use topical pain relievers or take nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs to reduce pain, swelling, and inflammation.
  • Protect: Protect the joint with a brace or wrap.
  • Compress: Compress the joint using an elastic wrap.
  • Elevate: Elevate the joint above the level of your heart.

    • back Pain

  • Apply ice for the first 48–72 hours, then use heat. Cold therapy can reduce pain, swelling, and inflammation. Heat therapy can help blood, oxygen, and nutrients flow to your muscles to promote healing and pain relief.

    • weakness

  • Check the person’s temperature, blood pressure, blood sugar, and oxygen levels if you have the equipment and know how to use it. This can help assess the severity of the condition and the need for medical attention.
  • If the person has low blood sugar, give them something sweet to eat or drink, such as juice, candy, or glucose tablets. This can help raise their blood sugar and improve their energy level.
  • If the person has low blood pressure, give them some salted water or a sports drink to drink. This can help increase their blood pressure and prevent dehydration.
  • If the person has a fever, give them some paracetamol or ibuprofen to lower their temperature and reduce inflammation. Make sure they drink plenty of fluids and rest.

    • Loss of consciousness

  • Check for a pulse, breathing, and a clear airway.
  • If the person is breathing and has no injuries, position them on their back with their legs raised about 12 inches above their heart.
  • Bend the person's top leg so the hip and knee are at right angles. Gently tilt their head back.
  • If breathing or pulse stops, roll the person onto their back and begin CPR.
